Jetliner

Sitting awkwardly on a rock
Speckled with tiny white patches
Of dried lichen — even the inanimate lives around here —

I am lulled to trance by the wind
Despite the stone pressing into the bony
Parts of my arse, which I think to myself
Is a bush comfort I should be grateful for

And lose myself again in my quest for Lingshan
And a vacant hotel room somewhere in southern China
In the 80s, surrounded by streams of bikes
And gauche pictures of pretty women

Advertising clothes, cosmetics and birth control
I don’t notice the incongruity
Of the pouring rain with the mat of pine needles
That crunch, crunch, crunch

Under my feet until the wind
Becomes a whine and the whine
Becomes more insistent, disturbing
The inherent politeness of this place

And whatever sense of Chinese decorum I may have inadvertently acquired
And I rise, and rise

From the dense pages of my book
For my eyes to meet the fern
Between twin engines

That effortlessly split the forest canopy:
I think it was Air New Zealand.
