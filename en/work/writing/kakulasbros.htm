Remembering the Kakulas Bros Warehouse Fire

A year ago now, the day before Valentine’s, Perth was set ablaze by the sight of thick black smoke rising from somewhere in the heart of the city.

My boyfriend and I had been driving up the Kwinana Freeway to have lunch with a friend at an Afghan restaurant, when we started seeing ambulances and fire engines racing towards the city. They came one after the other, lights blazing, and the tension rose in my chest as I realized something big must be happening. We could only speculate on what it was until the road rose up a little, and we saw the single plume rising from somewhere behind the Perth skyline.

My heart sunk immediately. My boyfriend suggested that it could be a bushfire, but I knew from looking how acrid and concentrated the smoke was, and where it was coming from, that it could only be a building fire somewhere in the city itself. It was hard not to imagine the worst as the emergency vehicles continued to race past, lights blazing. The smoke loomed over us as we approached the city, and on the bridge to Kings Park we saw a group of Woodside protesters dressed as Grim Reapers, dangling a banner about the climate crisis.

We wondered aloud whether they were aware of the disaster right behind them. Entering the Mitchell Freeway, we started to see ambulances racing down southbound too. We turned the car around, told our friend we would be late, and went into the city to investigate.

We parked in Northbridge as close as we safely could and walked a short distance on foot, to find the usually quiet residential area by William Street engulfed in grey, awful smelling smoke.

Firefighters and news crews were barely visible in the smoke and steam, as local onlookers huddled in the shadow of the Perth Mosque, covering their faces with their clothes.

The wind was blowing the smoke towards us and the firefighters told us to get back, so we headed back down a neighbouring street, where we could see the cranes spraying water on the blaze only metres away. Police officers were going door to door, informing residents they needed to evacuate.

We reached the southern end of the street, where we took shelter in what had obviously become the media encampment. TV news journalists from the major channels and their crews looked on at the unfolding pandemonium.

Evan Kakulas, the third generation son of Kakulas Bros, was discussing the situation with the emergency services in his shirt and shorts. Fire units continued to arrive on scene, and firefighters carried oxygen and other equipment toward the burning warehouse.

There were dozens of fire, police, ambulance and city ranger vehicles crowded into the closed roads around the site. I’d never seen so many emergency vehicles in my life. Fire engines and Specialised Equipment Tenders were coming from places as far as Gosnells and Murdoch.

A media representative for DFES came out to give us a pre-briefing, saying they would be available for questions in half an hour. Preliminarily he informed us that the blaze was contained but not under control, was of an unknown cause, and all warehouse employees had been accounted for. Relieved that there had been no casualties, and with friends waiting for us, we concluded our investigation and went up to meet out friends for the late lunch. The Kabuli pulao was excellent, as always. On the way home that evening, we happened to pass a DFES command vehicle that was also driving back, the fire having been successfully extinguished.

I found myself walking past the site again four months later, and by this time there was nothing left to see of what had been the old, dingy, graffiti-covered warehouse.

With the fortunate fact that no lives were lost, the incident has been quickly forgotten, and Kakulas Brothers continues operation from its prominent storefront near the corner of William St and Roe. It is unlikely that many people will be remembering the Kakulas Warehouse fire one year on, but on the day, it could be seen all over Perth, and you could see everywhere the looks of uncertain dread as people imagined the possibilities.
