The Renters (Pt. I)

The line stretched long and far back in the heat of the mid-day Sun, snaking its way between overgrown utes and little boxed off lawns that didn’t look fit to hold a flamingo.

“God, it’s hot today”, said Mitch, wiping away the forming beads of sweat on his brow as he walked to join the back of the line with his partner, Michelle.

“We should’ve gone earlier.”

Michelle, water bottle in hand and wearing a wide- brimmed hat of the kind you see people wear in trips to the Zoo, looked at his overly exposed figure with a kind of mildly disappointed compassion.

“This was the only slot they had left today; c’mon, it won’t be too long.”

They went around a corner and took their place against a tall blue Colourbond fence, huddling as the others were against a slim sliver of shadow.

“At least we’ve got a bit of shade here.”

Along the street the house was on, there were more real estate signs than trees, and the few trees that were, were little more than saplings that the sunlight pierced with ease. A short stumpy hedge along one of the homes was the closest thing there was to an approximation of shelter, and a group of people huddled under it like a cluster of refugees.

There were all kinds of people waiting in the line, from old to young, and even a few children. There were Indian and African women in their colourful clothes, suit-wearing businessmen with wives in form fitting dresses that matched the colour of their ties, middle-aged bogans with Southern Cross tattoos and KISS singlets, a few tradies still in their bright yellow work-shirts, and even a couple of goths with colourful highlights in their hair, and eyes adorned with black shadow.

All looked uncomfortable to some greater or lesser degree, as some chatted and laughed, some tried to keep cool by fanning themselves, a few Mums watched over thankfully quiet babies, and most were on their phones, scrolling through Insta or rechecking the rental listing.

That is what Michelle was doing as the line crawled forward at a glacial pace, while the melting ferocity of the Sun remained unrelenting. Looking over her shoulder as he tried to distract himself, Mitch piped up:

“Five hundred and fifty a week for a place like this. It’s ridiculous, it’s basically my entire paycheck.”

“Yeah it’s crazy, but it’s still cheaper than most these days. We’ve got my pay so we’ll be OK. I feel bad for the people doing this by themselves, I don’t know how…”

“… yeah …”, Mitch agreed solemnly.

“It’s been hard enough for us to find a place, I can’t imagine how some of these people are going to get by.”

His eyes drifted along the line, as if to say that some of the people standing with them had a tragic fate in store. It had been impossible not to notice the change in Perth over the past few years. The spectre of homelessness loomed ever present over the city, as the doorways of the damned became full of people in ragged beds, and tents started popping up in hidden places in beaches and parks. The Canning Bridge intersection had become a bazaar of desperation as people sold their window washing services, and it was impossible to travel far without seeing signs of misery.

That spectre had seemed something distant to Mitch and Michelle, but now that their end of lease was coming, with them still not having a new place to stay, the haunting fear loomed ever so slightly closer.

The heat continued to bear down on them relentlessly, as the skin on Mitch’s arms started to turn red and the line continued to creep slowly forwards.

“At least it’s a new place.”

Up ahead there were sudden gasps.

As people stood back, it became clear that one of the Indian women had collapsed on the footpath.

“Oh my God”

Armed with her water bottle, Michelle went forward to assist. She crouched down beside the woman with a few others, including a man who seemed to be her husband.

“My God, wake up darling, wake up!”

Michelle took a cloth from her bag and damped some water onto it, placing it on the woman’s sun- blanched forehead.

At this moment a woman strode forward, saying “Hey, I’m a nurse, what’s going on?”

She told the crowd to stand back slightly, as the woman turned her head slowly, opening her eyes with a pained expression. Michelle watched as the nurse and the man gently helped her sit up, explaining to her that she had just fainted, and moved her to a nearby brick wall.

The line had barely moved as Michelle went back to Mitch to resume their long wait. The Sun continued to blaze down on them, unmoved by their plight.

After about twenty more minutes of waiting, Michelle and Mitch finally reached the head of the queue, just after one in the afternoon, where they were greeted by the broad grin of the rental salesman, teeth porcelain white like a freshly cleaned toilet bowl.

“Hi nice to meet you I’m David”, he said as he confidently thrust his hand forward into the gap between them.

Not a hair on his head was out of place, in contrast to the growing pools of sweat under Michelle’s arms, while Mitch was well and truly starting to look like a slice of watermelon left out on a table just a bit too long.

Michelle took the offered shake, introducing herself and her partner.

“Sorry about the wait, we’ve had a lot of interest in this property.”, the agent said as he led them said inside.

“Oh no, looks like you got a bit sunburnt!”

“Yeah… I probably should’ve used some sunscreen…” Mitch sheepishly admitted.

“There’s some water over there if you need it.”; the agent gestured to a water cooler with some paper cups.

“As you can see, it is a very new property, with modern facilities throughout; it was built only six months ago, and the landlord intends to use it as an investment property. It has two bedrooms, two bathrooms including the master on-suite, and a comfortable open plan living area – if you have any questions just let me know – hi my name is David, nice to meet you…”

Almost in a singular motion, the agent swung around and began repeating his mantra to the next couple.

Left to look around, Mitch and Michelle went into the strangely dog-legged combined living and kitchen area: the double carport had obviously taken a lot of the floor-plan.

The kitchen was tucked away in a little nook, and out the windows either side were magnificent views of grey fences, with a sliver of blue sky for optimism.

“It feels like a prison”, Mitch confided safely out of earshot.

“At least it doesn’t have mould in the walls like your old place.”

“At least I could actually see some nature in my old place! … It was a shit-hole, though.”

“It was. I know it’s a bit small, but it’s as good as we can get these days.”

As good as we can get was starting to become a mantra of its own, words to live by, but to live was becoming hard enough.

“Mitch!”, Michelle shouted from her office.

“I just got an email, we got approved for that place down south!”

“Fuck yeah! Jesus, it came just in time…”

Mitch could not finish his sentence before being taken up by Michelle’s arms. Her tears were already flowing.

“We’ve got a place, we don’t need to worry anymore! They’ve even offered us a 12 month lease – 12 months!”

“Aww, you’re gonna make me start crying too!”

The two stood amidst the packing boxes in a long embrace. They were safe, for at least a while longer.
